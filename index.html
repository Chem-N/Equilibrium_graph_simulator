Mathematical Principles of the Simulation

1. Definitions

  • Let \([A]_t\) be the concentration of species A at time step \(t\).
  • Let \([B]_t\) be the concentration of species B at time step \(t\).
  • Let \(k_1\) be the rate constant of the forward reaction (\(A \rightarrow B\)).
  • Let \(k_2\) be the rate constant of the backward reaction (\(B \rightarrow A\)).

2. Continuous Rate Equations (Chemical Kinetics)

For the reversible reaction \(\text{A} \rightleftharpoons \text{B}\), the overall rate of change for each species is determined by the difference between the forward and backward reaction rates.

Rate of change of [A]: The concentration of A decreases due to the forward reaction and increases due to the backward reaction.
\(\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -k_1[A]_t + k_2[B]_t\)
Rate of change of [B]: The concentration of B increases due to the forward reaction and decreases due to the backward reaction.
\(\frac{d[B]}{dt} = k_1[A]_t - k_2[B]_t\)

3. Discrete Time Simulation (Euler's Method)

Because computers calculate in discrete steps rather than continuous time, this simulation uses a step-by-step numerical approximation (Euler's method) where each time step (\(\Delta t\)) equals \(1\) cycle.

The concentration of a species at the next time step (\(t+1\)) is equal to its current concentration plus the net change that occurred during that step:

Concentration of A at time \(t+1\):
\([A]_{t+1} = [A]_t + \left( -k_1[A]_t + k_2[B]_t \right)\)

\([A]_{t+1} = [A]_t - k_1[A]_t + k_2[B]_t\)
Concentration of B at time \(t+1\):
\([B]_{t+1} = [B]_t + \left( k_1[A]_t - k_2[B]_t \right)\)

\([B]_{t+1} = [B]_t + k_1[A]_t - k_2[B]_t\)

4. The Equilibrium State

Dynamic equilibrium is reached when the concentrations of macroscopic species no longer change. Mathematically, this means the rate of change is zero:

\(\frac{d[A]}{dt} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d[B]}{dt} = 0\)

Setting the net rate equation to zero gives us:

\(0 = -k_1[A]_{eq} + k_2[B]_{eq}\)

\(k_1[A]_{eq} = k_2[B]_{eq}\)

By rearranging this equation, we can derive the equilibrium constant (\(K_c\)) strictly from the kinetic rate constants:

\(K_c = \frac{[B]_{eq}}{[A]_{eq}} = \frac{k_1}{k_2}\)

This proves that no matter what the initial concentrations of A and B are, the system will eventually stabilize at a ratio of \(k_1 / k_2\).
